What is OCD?

Obsessive–Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is a mental health condition involving unwanted, intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors or mental acts (compulsions). These can cause distress and interfere with daily life.

OCD is not just about being neat or organized. It can affect anyone, and everyone’s experience is unique.

This website is for education and self-reflection, not a diagnosis.

OCD Features

  • Obsessive thoughts
  • Repetitive behaviors
  • Mental rituals
  • Distress & anxiety

Common OCD Themes

Everyone’s experience is unique, but some patterns are common.

Contamination & Cleaning

Frequent washing, cleaning, or avoiding germs due to fear of contamination.

Checking

Repeatedly checking doors, appliances, or tasks to prevent harm or mistakes.

Ordering & Symmetry

Arranging items or actions until they feel "just right" or symmetrical.

Intrusive Thoughts

Unwanted, distressing thoughts or images that are hard to control.

Hoarding

Difficulty discarding items, leading to clutter and distress.

Reassurance Seeking

Frequently asking others for reassurance to reduce anxiety.

Coping Strategies & Skills

CBT & ERP: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Exposure & Response Prevention (ERP) are effective treatments for OCD. They help you face fears and reduce compulsive behaviors.

Grounding techniques: Simple exercises to bring your focus to the present moment.

Journaling: Writing down thoughts and triggers can help you understand patterns and progress.

Label thoughts as thoughts, not facts
Remind yourself that having a thought doesn’t make it true or dangerous.
Delay compulsions by a few minutes
Practice waiting before acting on a compulsion. The urge may lessen over time.
Practice breathing exercises
Slow, deep breaths can help calm anxiety and bring you back to the present.
Reach out to a trusted person
Talking to someone you trust can provide support and perspective.
